Greatest 20th Century American Films

Citizen Kane (1941)
The theme of "Citizen Kane" revolves around the elusive nature of truth and the complexity of human identity as seen through the enigmatic life of Charles Foster Kane.
Casablanca (1942)
The theme of "Casablanca" centers on sacrifice, love, and moral ambiguity amidst the backdrop of World War II, highlighting the struggle between personal desires and greater obligations to humanity.
The Godfather (1972)
The theme of "The Godfather" revolves around power, family, loyalty, and the corrupting influence of ambition in the criminal underworld.
Gone with the Wind (1939)
The theme of "Gone with the Wind" encompasses the transformation of societal norms and personal resilience amidst the backdrop of the American Civil War and Reconstruction era, exploring themes of love, survival, and the consequences of holding onto the past.
Lawrence of Arabia (1962)
The theme of "Lawrence of Arabia" delves into the complexities of identity, imperialism, and the blurred lines between heroism and hubris as seen through the enigmatic figure of T.E. Lawrence during World War I.
The Wizard of Oz (1939)
The theme of "The Wizard of Oz" revolves around the journey of self-discovery, friendship, and the realization that the true power lies within oneself, as exemplified by Dorothy's adventures in the fantastical land of Oz.

Impact of Cinema

The impact of cinema on the 20th century was profound and multifaceted, shaping societies, cultures, and even political landscapes across the globe. Since its inception in the late 19th century, cinema rapidly evolved into a dominant form of mass entertainment and a powerful medium for storytelling, communication, and artistic expression. In the early 20th century, silent films captivated audiences worldwide, transcending language barriers and offering a window into different cultures and experiences. The rise of Hollywood in the United States turned cinema into a booming industry, with stars like Charlie Chaplin, Mary Pickford, and Buster Keaton becoming iconic figures. During times of war and conflict, cinema played a pivotal role in propaganda efforts, shaping public opinion and rallying support for various causes. The Golden Age of Hollywood in the 1930s and 1940s saw the emergence of classics that continue to resonate with audiences today, such as "Gone with the Wind," "Casablanca," and "Citizen Kane." As technology advanced, the introduction of sound and color revolutionized the cinematic experience, further expanding its reach and influence. The post-war era witnessed the rise of independent cinema movements worldwide, challenging traditional narratives and pushing the boundaries of storytelling. The 1960s and 1970s saw a wave of groundbreaking films that addressed social issues, cultural revolutions, and existential themes, reflecting the turbulence of the times. Directors like Ingmar Bergman, Federico Fellini, and Akira Kurosawa revolutionized filmmaking with their innovative techniques and profound storytelling. The advent of television posed new challenges to the film industry, but cinema continued to thrive, embracing new genres and technologies such as special effects and CGI. In the late 20th century, the globalization of cinema led to the emergence of new cinematic powerhouses beyond Hollywood, including Bollywood in India and Nollywood in Nigeria, further diversifying the medium and amplifying its impact on a global scale. Overall, cinema in the 20th century transcended entertainment to become a mirror of society, reflecting its triumphs, struggles, and aspirations while shaping the collective imagination of generations to come.

Academy Museum preserving Film History

The Academy Museum of Motion Pictures is the largest museum in the United States devoted to the arts, sciences, and artists of moviemaking. Global in outlook and grounded in the unparalleled collections and expertise of the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences, the Academy Museum offers exceptional exhibitions and programs that illuminate the world of cinema. They are immersive and dynamic and tell the many stories of the movies—their art, technology, artists, history, and social impact—through a variety of diverse and engaging voices. The Academy Museum tells complete stories of moviemaking—celebratory, educational, and sometimes critical or uncomfortable.
